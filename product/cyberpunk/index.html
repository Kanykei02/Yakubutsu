    Cyberpunk is an important direction of science fiction, which describes how digital technologies, cybernetics and virtual reality have entered the lives of people of the future as closely as possible.
    The genre originated in the 80s of the last century, first in literature, and then spread to other types of art. Writer and editor Gardner Dozois described cyberpunk with the phrase “high tech, low life,” which meant high technology combined with “low” life. However, the heyday of the cyberpunk movement did not last long. So, at least in the early 90s, said its founders - William Gibson and Bruce Sterling. Critics and journalists have come to the same conclusion. But then Neil Stevenson entered the literary arena, whose novels "Avalanche" and "Diamond Age" were immediately dubbed post-cyberpunk. Formally, his works did not differ too much from the previous books of the genre, it was the same cyberpunk - but in a more convincing and close to reality version.

    Several decades before cyberpunk formed into a separate literary movement, precursor works appeared that largely anticipated the future genre aesthetics.

    One of them is the novel “Tiger! Tiger "(in another version" My goal is the stars ") Alfred Bester. The book was published in 1956 and told about the only surviving member of the spacecraft, Gulliver Foyle. The book contains a huge number of fantastic ideas that became widespread much later. Widespread high technology, moral decline, powerful corporations conspiring - all this later became attributes of cyberpunk.

    Another equally important forerunner of the genre is the novel Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep? Philip Dick. Published in 1968, the novel described a future in which, despite the development of technology and robotics, humanity was in decline. And Blade Runner's adaptation of the novel by Ridley Scott largely defined the visual style of cyberpunk. William Gibson was even afraid to watch it in fear of the fact that the film revealed the ideas of cyber fiction much better than he did in his "Neuromancer".

    The name for the genre was coined by Gardner Dozois when he came across a short story by Bruce Batke called Cyberpunk. The story itself hardly fit the definition of a genre, but the word itself stuck. It screamed about the underground, which, in general, was the nascent cyberpunk.Although some individual short stories and novels have appeared before, the full-fledged birth of the genre came with the release of William Gibson's novel "Neuromancer" (1984), and its author was dubbed "the father of cyberspace." He then developed the story into a trilogy. In 1985 Sterling released Schizmatrix. These books became classics of the genre and inspired a whole wave of other authors to start writing in this genre.

    The cyberpunk genre was a mixture of science fiction, dystopia and noir. The classic conflict revolved around the juxtaposition of the street — lone hackers, criminals, or beggars — and powerful corporations. It is not for nothing that the word "punk" was put into a protest meaning.

    The aesthetics of a grim neon future, in which mass cybernetics came into use, remained attractive.
    Only a special "underground protest charge" was no longer required - and this was proved by Neil Stevenson, whose novel "Avalanche" was a breath of fresh air and in many respects relaunched the cyberpunk movement. Unlike Gibson and Sterling, Stevenson was firsthand familiar with computer technology and programming, and therefore initially had a more sober view of the future of technology. In Avalanche, the author depicted a world divided into countless corporations. In a leisurely, ironic, figurative language, he told the story of "tomorrow." Almost 30 years later, the novel has not fully materialized into reality, but many things - like mobile phones and the Internet - have become commonplace. In Avalanche, Stevenson described a virtual reality called the Metaverse. Any person on the planet can enter it from almost anywhere (if there is equipment). Leaving aside the spread of a virtual virus that is dangerous for the real world, the Metaverse in its essence anticipated the emergence of social networks and modern VR technologies.
